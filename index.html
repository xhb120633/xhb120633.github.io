About

Computational approaches to understanding human cognition and improving AI systems.

I am a Ph.D. student in Psychology at Georgia Institute of Technology, specializing in computational cognitive science. Before Georgia Tech, I completed an M.A. at the University of Arizona, and previously spent three years at Peking University as a full-time research assistant in CBCS. I received my undergraduate degree in Human Resource Management from Southwestern University of Finance and Economics in China. I am currently visiting Tom Griffiths' CoCoSci Lab at Princeton University.

My research focuses on using large language models (LLMs) and computational tools to understand decision-making and learning, especially through think-aloud protocols. I am also interested in AI behavior, interpretability, human-AI interaction, and using human data to improve model alignment and scientific discovery.

I co-founded MindRL-Hub, a community supporting interdisciplinary reinforcement learning research in psychology and neuroscience.

Research Highlights

Selected themes and current directions.

Think-Aloud Data and LLMs

I study how think-aloud verbal reports can be modeled computationally to reveal latent cognitive processes in decision-making and learning tasks. This line of work explores scalable alternatives to subjective manual coding and uses LLMs to interpret and predict behavior from language.

Reverse Engineering Human Thought

This direction asks whether underlying thought patterns can be reconstructed from behavior and language data. The broader goal is to establish a stronger bridge between observable actions and latent cognitive mechanisms, while making advanced model behavior more interpretable to humans.

Human Insights for Better AI

I investigate how methods from cognitive science can improve AI systems and, conversely, how AI systems can support theory building in psychology and neuroscience.
